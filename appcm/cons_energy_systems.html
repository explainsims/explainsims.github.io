Lab Guide
System: Block Only

The system boundary encloses only the block. Both gravity and friction act as external forces — they do work on the block, changing its kinetic energy. The block has no internal potential energy.

Forces: Gravity (ext) Friction (ext)

Work-Energy Theorem:
Kf = Ki + Wnet
Kf = Wgravity + Wfriction
K = — J

As the block slides down, gravity does positive work (increases KE) and friction does negative work (decreases KE). The net work equals the change in kinetic energy. The block speeds up as long as |Wg| > |Wf|.

System: Block + Earth

Expanding the boundary to include the Earth brings gravity inside the system. Gravity is no longer an external force doing work — instead, it stores energy as gravitational potential energy Ug. Only friction remains external.

Forces: Gravity → Ug (int) Friction (ext)

Conservation with External Work:
ΔEmech = Wfriction
ΔK + ΔUg = Wfriction
(K + Ug)f − (K + Ug)i = Wf
ΔEmech = — J

The total mechanical energy (K + Ug) is not conserved because friction removes energy from the system. The ghost line on the total bar shows the initial mechanical energy — watch it decrease as friction does negative work.

System: Block + Earth + Surface

The largest system boundary includes the block, Earth, and the ramp surface. Now both gravity (Ug) and friction (Eth) are internal. This is an isolated system — no external work is done.

Forces: Gravity → Ug (int) Friction → Eth (int)

Conservation of Energy (Isolated System):
ΔEsys = 0
ΔK + ΔUg + ΔEth = 0
K + Ug + Eth = constant
Esys = — J

Total system energy is perfectly conserved! The energy bars show Ug converting to K and Eth. Thermal energy is irreversible — it cannot spontaneously convert back to mechanical energy, making friction a dissipative (non-conservative) force.
