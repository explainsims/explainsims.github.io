Power: Rate of Energy Transfer
Definition of Power
3.5.A.1
P = dE/dt

Power is the rate at which energy changes with respect to time. It describes how quickly energy is transferred or transformed — not how much total energy, but how fast it flows.

The SI unit is the Watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.

Average Power
3.5.A.2 & 3
P_avg = ΔE/Δt = W/Δt

For a finite time interval, average power equals the total energy transferred (or work done) divided by the time interval. This is the algebraic (non-calculus) version used most frequently in introductory problems.

If a machine does 600 J of work in 3 s, its average power is 200 W.

Instantaneous Power (Calculus)
3.5.A.4
P_inst = dW/dt

For variable forces, power varies with time. Instantaneous power is the time derivative of Work — the slope of a W vs. t graph at any given instant.

If W(t) = 3t², then P(t) = dW/dt = 6t Watts. Power increases linearly with time in this case.

Power via Force Vector
3.5.A.5
P = F·v = Fv cos(θ)

Power equals the dot product of the force and velocity vectors. Only the component of force parallel to the velocity contributes to power.

When θ = 90°, cos(90°) = 0, so a purely perpendicular force (like centripetal force) does zero work and delivers zero power.

AP Exam Tip — FRQ Strategy

In Free Response Questions (FRQs), if you are asked to calculate power from a graph of Force vs. Position, remember that area is Work. You might need to use chain rule or kinematic equations to relate position to time to find Power.
