The Physics of Banked Tracks
How Banked Curves Work

When a vehicle travels around a curve, it undergoes circular motion, requiring a net centripetal force directed toward the center. On a flat road, only friction provides this force.

By banking the curve at angle θ, the normal force (N), always perpendicular to the surface, gains a horizontal component that contributes to the centripetal force — reducing reliance on friction.

Key Forces

Gravity (mg): Weight of the vehicle, always acting straight down.

Normal Force (N): Perpendicular to the road surface. On a banked track, it has both vertical and horizontal components.

Friction (fₛ): Parallel to the road surface. Acts down the slope if speed is too high, up the slope if speed is too low.

Centripetal Force (F꜀): The net horizontal force (N·sin θ ± fₛ·cos θ) = mv²/r. Not a separate force.

Understanding the Speed Limits

Ideal Speed: At this speed, N's horizontal component exactly provides the centripetal force. No friction needed — most stable condition.

Min & Max Speeds: Set by maximum static friction (fₛ = μₛN). Below minimum, car slides down the bank. Above maximum, car slides up. Adjust the sliders to see how angle and friction define this safe range.
